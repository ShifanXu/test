Research

Architectures, memory systems, and fault tolerance for quantum computing.

Research Areas

Quantum Random Access Memory Architecture

I study how quantum random access memory should be designed as a practical subsystem inside a larger fault-tolerant machine. This includes questions of bandwidth, sharing, routing, organization, defect tolerance, and structured data access. More broadly, I am interested in when quantum memory architectures are actually useful at system scale, and what architectural assumptions make them practical.

Fault-Tolerant Magic-State Preparation

I work on how non-Clifford resources should be prepared under realistic fault-tolerant architectural constraints. This includes magic-state injection, distillation, scheduling, and subsystem integration, especially in settings where code structure and connectivity strongly affect cost. A recurring theme is that magic-state preparation should be viewed as an architectural problem rather than only a protocol-design problem.

Error-Correction-Aware Architecture

A broader theme in my work is that error correction cannot be treated as a black box beneath the architecture. Different codes expose different constraints on layout, routing, movement, and resource sharing, and those differences should shape architectural decisions from the start. I am interested in how code choices and system organization can be coordinated so that fault tolerance becomes a design principle rather than a downstream constraint.

Algorithm-Architecture Co-Design

I am also interested in settings where quantum algorithms and architectures should be optimized together. This includes problems where data access structure, fault-tolerant resource costs, and workload organization all influence what architecture is appropriate. The goal is to move beyond a clean separation between software and hardware layers, and instead study quantum systems through the interaction between algorithms, subsystems, and implementation constraints.

Research Perspective

Across these projects, I am interested in the systems bottlenecks that separate abstract quantum algorithms from deployable large-scale machines. My work tries to connect architectural abstractions with realistic error-corrected implementations and quantitative resource tradeoffs.

The common theme is that scalable quantum computing will depend not only on better codes or better devices, but also on better subsystem interfaces, memory organizations, and full-stack evaluation methodologies. I am especially interested in questions where architectural structure changes what is realistically implementable.
